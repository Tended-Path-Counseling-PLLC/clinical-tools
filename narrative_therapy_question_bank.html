Practice 1
Externalizing the Problem
The foundational move of Narrative Therapy: separating the person from the problem. "The person is not the problem — the problem is the problem." Externalization opens space between the client's identity and the difficulty, making it possible to examine the problem rather than inhabit it. This overlaps meaningfully with ACT defusion and IFS "unblending."
Clinical note: Externalization isn't just a naming exercise — it's an invitation to shift the ontological relationship with the problem. The client begins to locate the problem as something with influence over them, rather than something they are. Hold the metaphor lightly; follow the client's language.
Naming and Locating the Problem
If this thing you've been carrying had a name — not a clinical label, but a name that fit how it actually feels — what would you call it?
Anchor
When did [problem] first show up in your life?
Anchor
If [problem] were a character or a presence — not a person necessarily, but something with a quality or texture — what would it be like?
Where does [problem] tend to live in your body, when it shows up?
Does [problem] come and go, or does it feel like it's always there in some form?
Understanding Its Voice and Agenda
What does [problem] tell you about who you are?
Anchor
What does [problem] want you to believe about yourself? About your future?
How does [problem] recruit you into its way of seeing things?
What does [problem] make it hard to see or remember about yourself?
When [problem] has the most influence, what does it get you to do? What does it steer you away from?
Is there anything [problem] has tried to convince you is permanently true about you?
Relational Effects
How has [problem] affected your relationships with people you care about?
Has [problem] gotten between you and anyone important to you?
What do you imagine others see when [problem] is running the show?
ACT/IFS bridge: Externalization language maps naturally onto IFS ("the part that carries shame") and ACT defusion ("the story your mind is telling you"). You don't need to switch frameworks — just notice where the language overlaps and let it deepen.
Practice 2
Mapping the Influence
After externalizing, Narrative Therapy maps the problem's reach — how much territory it occupies across the client's life. This includes relative influence mapping: where the problem has power, where the client has power, and what has changed over time. It builds a precise picture before looking for openings.
Clinical note: White and Epston originally called this "relative influence questioning" — mapping both the influence of the problem on the person and the influence of the person on the problem. Both directions matter. Don't just catalog losses; look for where the client's influence is also present.
Scope and Territory
In what areas of your life has [problem] had the most influence?
Anchor
Has [problem] always had this much territory, or has its hold grown or shrunk over time?
Are there areas of your life where [problem] hasn't been able to get a foothold?
On a scale from 0 to 10, how much influence does [problem] have over your day-to-day life right now? Where was that number five years ago?
Costs and Constraints
What has [problem] cost you?
Anchor
What has [problem] kept you from doing or becoming?
What does [problem] require of you to maintain its grip?
What have you had to give up or set aside because of [problem]?
Who else in your life has been affected by [problem]'s presence?
History and Context
Were there times when [problem] had even more influence than it does now? What was different then?
Looking back, what conditions seemed to give [problem] the most room to grow?
Did [problem] seem to come from somewhere specific — a relationship, a period of time, a set of messages you received?
How do the stories you've been told — by family, culture, or experience — help keep [problem] in place?
Client's Influence on the Problem
Have there been times when you've pushed back against [problem], even a little? What did that look like?
Anchor
What do you know about [problem] that it maybe doesn't want you to know?
Are there things you've learned to do — or not do — that limit how much space [problem] gets?
Practice 3
Unique Outcomes
Unique outcomes (also called "sparkling moments" or "exceptions") are moments when the dominant problem story did not fully determine what happened. They are often small, overlooked, or dismissed by the client as flukes — which is exactly why they matter. These are the raw material of re-authoring.
Clinical note: Don't rush to the meaning of unique outcomes before establishing what happened. The landscape of action (facts, sequence, who/what/when) comes first. Only then move into the landscape of identity (what it means, what it says about the person). Premature meaning-making is thin; thick description builds lasting alternative stories.
Finding the Exception
Have there been times — even brief ones — when [problem] expected to take over, but didn't get everything it wanted?
Anchor
When was the last time you did something that [problem] wouldn't have predicted?
Anchor
Tell me about a moment when [problem] showed up, but you still managed to do something that mattered to you.
Was there a time you almost went along with [problem]'s pull — but didn't go all the way?
Is there anyone in your life who has seen you stand outside of what [problem] usually pulls you toward?
Can you think of a time — even years ago — when you acted in a way that was closer to who you want to be?
Grounding the Moment (Landscape of Action)
Walk me through that — what actually happened, step by step?
Anchor
What did you do in that moment? What did you say, or not say?
Who else was there, or connected to that moment in some way?
What was happening right before you made that choice or took that step?
What prepared you, even without knowing it, for that moment?
Making It Significant
Is this the kind of thing you typically dismiss or explain away? What if you didn't?
What does it tell you that this happened — even once?
If I took this moment seriously — as a data point about who you are — what would it point toward?
Practice 4
Re-authoring
Re-authoring is the process of building an alternative, preferred story — one that is grounded in the client's actual experience, not invented. It thickens the alternative narrative by linking unique outcomes into a coherent story of identity, values, and agency. This is where NT does its deepest work.
Clinical note: The alternative story must be claimed by the client, not assigned by the therapist. Your role is to ask questions that draw it forward, not to name it for them. Rushing to "so you're actually a resilient person" collapses the process. Let the client arrive at the description through their own words.
Naming the Quality or Value
What would you call this quality — the one that showed up in that moment?
Anchor
What does that moment say about what matters to you — about what you're trying to protect or move toward?
Anchor
If you were to give a name to the version of you that showed up in that moment, what would it be?
Does what you just described fit with anything you already know to be true about yourself — or is it a surprise?
Where else in your life has this quality shown up, even quietly?
Tracing the Alternative Story Back
When you think about who taught you — directly or indirectly — to care about the things you care about, who comes to mind?
Anchor
Have there been other moments in your life — maybe much earlier — where this same quality was present?
What would the person you were at age ten have to say about the moment you just described?
Is there a version of this story that goes further back than [problem] — a story that [problem] interrupted?
Extending the Story Forward
If you were to write the next chapter of your life from inside this story — not the one [problem] would write — what would be in it?
Anchor
What would it look like to give this quality more room in the coming weeks?
What small step might be consistent with this story — something [problem] wouldn't expect?
Who else in your life might notice if you began living more from this story?
What might need to shift — in how you see yourself, or in what you do — for this story to have more weight than [problem]'s story?
ACT/IFS bridge: Re-authoring maps onto committed action in ACT — moving toward values-consistent behavior — and onto IFS unburdening of exiled parts. The preferred story isn't a cognitive reframe; it's an identity-level shift. Helping the client claim it in their own words makes it durable.
Practice 5
Scaffolding Conversations
Michael White's scaffolding framework distinguishes between the landscape of action (what happened — events, sequences, steps) and the landscape of identity/consciousness (what it means — intentions, values, beliefs, identity conclusions). Good re-authoring moves through both levels, not just one.
Landscape of Action Events, steps, sequences, who/what/when. What actually happened. Grounds the unique outcome in concrete experience.
Landscape of Identity Intentions, values, beliefs, desires, hopes. What the event means about who the person is. The "so what" of the unique outcome.
Landscape of Action Questions
Walk me through what happened — in order. What came first?
Anchor
What did you do? What did you say, or choose not to say?
Who else was involved — even indirectly?
What led up to that moment? What made it possible?
What happened after? What changed, even slightly?
If someone had been watching — a bystander who cared about you — what would they have seen?
Landscape of Identity Questions
What does it say about you as a person that you were able to do that?
Anchor
What does that action tell you about what you care about — what you value or want to protect?
Anchor
What does this moment suggest about what you believe — about yourself, about others, about what's possible?
If this quality had a name — something that reflected the intention behind what you did — what would it be?
Does this fit with who you've always known yourself to be underneath, or is it showing you something new?
What does this say about what kind of life you want to be living?
Double-Listening / Opening Space
I'm noticing something in what you just said — would it be okay to stay with it a moment?
Anchor
There's a word you used — [word] — and I'm curious what you mean by it.
Is there more to that than you've been able to put into words yet?
You said that almost as a throwaway — but I want to come back to it.
Clinical note: "Double listening" — attending simultaneously to the problem story and the glimmers of the alternative story — is a core NT therapist stance. It means holding both tracks: what [problem] says is true, and what else is also true about this person's experience.
Practice 6
Definitional Ceremony & Outsider Witness
Narrative Therapy uses real or imagined witnesses to thicken the alternative story. In formal settings this involves outsider witness groups; in individual therapy, you can work with imagined or remembered witnesses — people whose attention and perspective would make the preferred story more real and lasting.
Clinical note: The outsider witness practice is not about getting external validation — it's about using relationship as a vehicle for identity consolidation. The imagined witness holds something the client may not yet be able to hold alone. You are also, in some sense, functioning as a witnessing presence. How you respond to disclosures of the preferred story matters.
Identifying Meaningful Witnesses
Who in your life — past or present — would be least surprised to hear what you're discovering about yourself?
Anchor
Is there someone — living or not — whose witnessing of this shift would feel most meaningful to you?
Anchor
Who in your life has seen glimmers of this version of you, even if you dismissed it at the time?
Is there anyone you wish could see you doing this work right now?
Has anyone — a teacher, a friend, an unlikely person — ever said something to you that pointed toward this quality you're naming now?
Imagined Witnessing
If [person] could see you here today, doing this work — what do you imagine they would say?
Anchor
What would it mean to you if [person] knew about this shift?
What might [person] notice that you haven't let yourself fully see yet?
Imagine you're telling [person] about what happened — how does your voice change? What do you emphasize?
What does it do to you — to your sense of yourself — to imagine being seen this way?
Carrying the Witness Forward
Is there someone in your life you'd want to share this with — not to explain it, but just to have a witness?
How might it change things to let [person] see this version of you?
Is there a conversation you've been putting off that might look different if you were coming from this story?
Practice 7
Therapeutic Documents & Letters
Narrative Therapy has a rich tradition of therapeutic letters, declarations, and certificates that make the preferred story tangible. In individual practice, these can be collaboratively written or prompted in session and given as a concrete artifact. They extend the session into the client's everyday life.
Clinical note: Research by White and Epston suggested that a therapeutic letter can have the impact of several sessions — because it can be re-read. Written documents give the alternative story a physical, portable form. Even prompting the client to draft something orally in session (and then transcribing it with them) can be powerful.
Letter Prompts — From the Client's Preferred Self
If the version of you who is living more freely from [problem]'s grip could write a letter to your current self — what would it say?
Anchor
What would you want your future self to remember about this moment in your life?
If you wrote a letter to the you from five years ago — knowing what you know now — what would you want them to hear?
What would you want to say to someone earlier in this same struggle — someone you've never met?
Declarations and Statements of Position
If you were writing a declaration about where you stand in relation to [problem] right now — what would it say?
Anchor
What is your official position on [problem]? Has that position changed?
Is there anything you want to say to [problem] directly — on paper, in your own words?
What do you want to put on record about who you are — outside of what [problem] has said about you?
Certificates and Recognition
If we were marking this moment — the one where you stepped outside of [problem]'s story — what would we call it?
What milestone deserves recognition that you've been too close to see?
Is there a skill, capacity, or quality you've developed in this period that you want to name — and keep?
Practice 8
Opening Space & Deconstructive Questions
These questions slow things down and create room for complexity. They deconstruct taken-for-granted assumptions — including cultural narratives, inherited beliefs, and conclusions the client has drawn about themselves that may have gone unexamined. Particularly useful early in NT work and when dominant stories feel especially rigid.
Clinical note: Deconstructive questioning isn't the same as challenging or disputing. It's an invitation to look at the architecture of a belief — where it came from, who benefits from it, whether the client actually endorses it. The tone should feel curious and spacious, not confrontational.
Tracing the Source of Beliefs
Where did you first learn to see yourself that way?
Anchor
Who first told you that about yourself? Do you think they were right?
Anchor
Is that a conclusion you arrived at on your own, or one that was handed to you?
If someone who loved you had been the author of that story about you — would they have written it differently?
Do you fully agree with that conclusion, or is there a part of you that pushes back on it?
Cultural and Contextual Deconstruction
Are there messages you've received — from your family, culture, or community — that have made [problem] harder to escape?
Anchor
Who benefits from you seeing yourself this way?
Are there broader stories — about gender, success, worth, belonging — that seem to be feeding [problem]?
If you'd grown up with different messages about yourself, what might have been different?
What would it mean to lay down the story that was written for you — and write one you actually endorse?
Curiosity and Complexifying
I'm curious — how did you come to see it that way?
Is there more to that than you've put into words yet?
If I asked someone who knew you well whether that's completely true — what would they say?
Does that description capture everything, or is there something that doesn't quite fit inside it?
What would you lose if you stopped believing that story? What might you gain?
ACT/IFS bridge: Deconstructive questioning in NT overlaps with ACT's cognitive defusion work and IFS's approach to examining the origins of a part's burdens. In all three frameworks, the goal is loosening the grip of a story — not arguing against it, but creating enough distance to ask "does this still serve me?"
