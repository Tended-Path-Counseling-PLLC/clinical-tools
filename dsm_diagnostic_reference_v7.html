Differential Diagnoses
Bipolar I or II Disorder+
Key distinguisher: A current or past manic or hypomanic episode rules out MDD and points to Bipolar. Carefully screen all depressed patients for prior periods of elevated/irritable mood, decreased sleep without fatigue, grandiosity, or impulsive/reckless behavior — clients often don't report these as problems.
Look for: hypersomnia and hyperphagia (more common in Bipolar depression), family history of Bipolar, early age of onset, multiple prior depressive episodes, antidepressant-induced activation or mood cycling.
Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)+
Key distinguisher: PDD requires depressed mood more days than not for ≥2 years (adults) with ≥2 additional symptoms — but symptom count and severity are lower than MDD. MDD can be superimposed on PDD ("double depression").
PDD often presents as chronic low-grade depression with the client describing "always feeling this way" — unlike MDD's episodic quality. Clients may not identify it as depression at all.
Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood+
Key distinguisher: Adjustment Disorder is a stress response — symptoms are directly linked to an identifiable stressor, don't meet full MDD criteria, and typically resolve within 6 months of stressor removal. If full MDD criteria are met, diagnose MDD regardless of precipitating stressor.
Medical Conditions / Substance Use+
Key distinguisher: Hypothyroidism, anemia, vitamin D/B12 deficiency, chronic pain, and sleep disorders can produce depressive symptoms. Always assess medical history and encourage PCP workup when MDD onset is atypical or treatment-resistant.
Substance use (alcohol especially) commonly co-occurs with or mimics depression. Assess temporal relationship — did depression precede or follow substance use?
Grief / Bereavement+
Key distinguisher: DSM-5-TR removed the bereavement exclusion. MDD can be diagnosed during grief if full criteria are met. Key differentiators: grief is typically wave-like, associated with the loss, and preserves capacity for positive emotions; MDD tends to be persistent, pervasive, and involves worthlessness/guilt unrelated to the loss.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~7% 12-month prevalence in adults; higher in women (1.5–3x). Peak onset: 20s–30s, though can occur at any age.
Course
Episodic; ~50% of first episodes are followed by additional episodes. Risk increases with each subsequent episode. Chronicity increases with comorbid anxiety, substance use, and personality features.
Clinical Notes
Comorbid anxiety is the norm, not the exception (~60%). Always screen for suicidality. Anhedonia, not just sad mood, is often the more functionally impairing symptom. Somatic complaints are a common presentation, particularly in some cultural contexts.
Common Comorbidities
GAD (~60%), Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety Disorder, PTSD, Persistent Depressive Disorder ("double depression"), Alcohol/Substance Use Disorders, Chronic pain, Borderline Personality Disorder.

Differential Diagnoses
Other Anxiety Disorders (Panic, Social, Specific Phobia)+
Key distinguisher: GAD involves worry across multiple domains. Panic Disorder centers on worry about having panic attacks; Social Anxiety is focused on social evaluation; Specific Phobia is tied to a discrete stimulus. GAD is diagnosed when anxiety is not restricted to these focal areas.
MDD with Anxious Distress+
Key distinguisher: Anxiety is extremely common in MDD. Both can co-occur. When anxiety and depressive symptoms are both present and both meet criteria, diagnose both. Use the "with anxious distress" specifier on MDD if anxiety symptoms are prominent but GAD criteria aren't fully met.
PTSD+
Key distinguisher: PTSD can include hypervigilance and anxiety that looks like GAD. The key differentiator is trauma exposure and symptom clusters (re-experiencing, avoidance). If anxiety is exclusively tied to trauma cues and hyperarousal, PTSD is the more accurate diagnosis — though both can co-occur.
OCD+
Key distinguisher: OCD involves intrusive, unwanted obsessions (often ego-dystonic) and compulsions to neutralize anxiety. GAD worry tends to be more ego-syntonic, feels like "normal" concern, and relates to real-life circumstances. OCD obsessions are often bizarre or taboo in content.
Medical Conditions / Substances+
Key distinguisher: Hyperthyroidism, cardiac arrhythmias, caffeine/stimulant use, and withdrawal states can produce anxiety symptoms. Always assess timeline and medical history. Anxiety that is exclusively explained by a medical condition = Anxiety Disorder Due to Another Medical Condition.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~2–3% 12-month prevalence; higher in women (2:1 ratio). Peak onset in 20s–30s but can present at any age. One of the most common anxiety disorders in outpatient settings.
Course
Chronic, waxing and waning. Many clients report feeling anxious "their whole life." Typically does not remit fully without treatment. Exacerbates under stress. Comorbidity with MDD is extremely common (~60%).
Clinical Notes
ACT and CBT (including worry postponement and cognitive restructuring) are well-supported. Clients often present with somatic complaints. Intolerance of uncertainty is a core maintaining factor.
Common Comorbidities
MDD (~60%), Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety Disorder, Specific Phobia, PTSD, OCD, Persistent Depressive Disorder, Somatic Symptom Disorder, Substance Use Disorders.
Panic Disorder
Anxiety Disorders · DSM-5-TR 300.01
DSM-5-TR 300.01 F41.0
Diagnostic Criteria
Criterion A — Recurrent Unexpected Panic Attacks
Panic Attack Symptoms ≥4 of 13 required; peaks within minutes
Criterion B — At Least One Attack Followed by ≥1 Month of Either:
Criteria C–D
The "unexpected" qualifier in Criterion A is essential — at least some attacks must occur without an obvious trigger. Expected panic attacks (cued by a known stimulus) can be a feature of other anxiety disorders but alone do not constitute Panic Disorder. Panic attacks themselves are a transdiagnostic symptom; the disorder requires the anticipatory anxiety and behavioral change of Criterion B.
Specifiers & Clinical Notes on Coding
No Formal DSM-5-TR Specifiers
Panic Disorder does not carry formal specifiers in DSM-5-TR beyond course (partial/full remission). Severity is documented clinically using frequency, impairment, and behavioral avoidance as guides.
In Partial / Full Remission
Partial: fewer than full criteria met; some anticipatory anxiety or behavioral change remains. Full: no significant symptoms for ≥2 months. Document course specifier when applicable.
Panic Attack Specifier (Other Diagnoses)
When panic attacks occur in the context of another disorder (e.g., MDD, PTSD, GAD) but do not meet Panic Disorder criteria, use the specifier "with panic attacks" on the primary diagnosis. Panic attacks are a transdiagnostic marker of severity.
Agoraphobia Comorbidity
Panic Disorder and Agoraphobia are coded as separate diagnoses in DSM-5-TR. If agoraphobic avoidance is present and meets criteria for Agoraphobia (F40.00), assign both diagnoses. Agoraphobia can also occur without a history of Panic Disorder.
Nocturnal Panic
A subset of clients experience nocturnal panic attacks — awakening from sleep in a full panic state. These are unexpected attacks, not nightmares, and support the Panic Disorder diagnosis.
Expected vs. Unexpected Attacks
Over the course of Panic Disorder, attacks may become cued (expected) as conditioning occurs. Diagnosis is still appropriate if the initial attacks were unexpected and Criterion B was met. Document shift in attack pattern in formulation.

Differential Diagnoses
Medical Conditions (Cardiac, Thyroid, Vestibular)+
Key distinguisher: Cardiac arrhythmias, hyperthyroidism, vestibular disorders, hypoglycemia, and pheochromocytoma can all produce symptoms identical to panic attacks. Rule out medical causes before diagnosing Panic Disorder, particularly for first presentations, new-onset panic in older adults, or atypical symptom profiles (e.g., predominantly cardiac or neurological symptoms). Encourage PCP workup including ECG, thyroid panel, and glucose.
Other Anxiety Disorders+
Key distinguisher: Panic attacks can occur across anxiety disorders. The key differentiator is the "unexpected" quality and Criterion B anticipatory anxiety/behavioral change. If attacks are exclusively cued by social situations → Social Anxiety. Exclusively cued by specific stimuli → Specific Phobia. Exclusively cued by trauma cues → PTSD. Panic Disorder requires attacks that are at least sometimes unexpected.
GAD+
Key distinguisher: Both GAD and Panic Disorder involve anxiety and worry. In Panic Disorder, worry is primarily about having future attacks and their consequences. In GAD, worry is diffuse and spans multiple life domains. Both can co-occur — indeed, comorbid GAD is common in Panic Disorder. Diagnose both when criteria for each are independently met.
MDD with Panic Attacks+
Key distinguisher: Panic attacks are extremely common in MDD. If panic attacks occur exclusively within a depressive episode and do not persist between episodes, they may be better characterized as a feature of MDD ("with panic attacks" specifier) rather than a co-occurring Panic Disorder. If the Criterion B anticipatory anxiety and behavioral change are present independent of the depressive episode, diagnose Panic Disorder separately.
Substance/Medication-Induced Anxiety+
Key distinguisher: Stimulant use (caffeine, cocaine, amphetamines) and withdrawal from CNS depressants (alcohol, benzodiazepines) can induce panic-like attacks. Assess temporal relationship carefully — attacks occurring exclusively during intoxication or withdrawal do not warrant a primary Panic Disorder diagnosis.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~2–3% 12-month prevalence; ~4.7% lifetime. Women are twice as likely as men to be diagnosed. Peak onset in late adolescence to early adulthood, though can appear at any age. ~10–15% of primary care patients with unexplained physical symptoms meet criteria.
Course
Variable and often chronic without treatment. Many clients have a waxing-and-waning course; some achieve sustained remission. Untreated, agoraphobic avoidance tends to expand over time. Early treatment significantly improves long-term prognosis.
Clinical Notes
CBT with interoceptive exposure is the gold standard. ACT is also well-supported. Clients often arrive post-ER visit with cardiac workup — psychoeducation about the physiology of panic is essential early in treatment. Avoidance is the primary maintenance factor. Somatic/body-based work fits well with Bethany's IFS/mind-body orientation.
Common Comorbidities
Agoraphobia (~50%), GAD, MDD (~60%), Social Anxiety Disorder, Specific Phobia, PTSD, Substance Use Disorders. High rates of medical service utilization prior to diagnosis.
Specific Phobia
Anxiety Disorders · DSM-5-TR 300.29
DSM-5-TR 300.29 F40.218 Animal F40.228 Natural Env. F40.23x Blood-Inj. F40.248 Situational F40.298 Other
Diagnostic Criteria
Criteria A–G
Multiple specific phobias are common; each should be assigned a separate diagnosis and ICD code. A client may present with both Specific Phobia and another anxiety disorder — these are not mutually exclusive. The 6-month duration criterion helps distinguish phobias from transient, developmentally normative fears.
Subtypes & Specifiers
Animal Type (F40.218)
Fear cued by animals or insects (e.g., spiders, dogs, snakes, insects). Typically onset in childhood. One of the most common types in general population.
Natural Environment Type (F40.228)
Fear cued by natural environment features: heights, storms, water. Height phobia (acrophobia) is one of the most prevalent. Typically childhood onset.
Blood-Injection-Injury (BII) Type (F40.23x)
Blood (F40.230), injections/transfusions (F40.231), other medical care (F40.232), injury (F40.233). BII is unique: the typical anxiety/sympathetic response is often followed by a vasovagal (fainting) response. Screen for this explicitly — treatment modifications are required (applied tension technique).
Situational Type (F40.248)
Public transportation, tunnels, bridges, elevators, flying, driving, enclosed spaces. Bimodal age of onset (childhood and mid-20s). Most common type in clinical settings among adults.
Other Type (F40.298)
Fear cued by other stimuli: choking, vomiting, contracting an illness, loud sounds, costumed characters. May not fit neatly into the above categories — use clinical judgment.
In Partial / Full Remission
Criteria may no longer be fully met with treatment (typically exposure-based). Document remission course when applicable. Full remission requires no significant fear/anxiety/avoidance for ≥2 months.

Differential Diagnoses
Panic Disorder / Agoraphobia+
Key distinguisher: If the client fears panic attacks in specific situations (e.g., enclosed spaces or crowds) and the primary driver is anticipation of panic rather than the situation itself, Panic Disorder with or without Agoraphobia is more accurate. Specific Phobia situational type is focused on the situation as the feared object; Agoraphobia involves fear of multiple situations linked by difficulty escaping or getting help during a panic attack.
Social Anxiety Disorder+
Key distinguisher: Social Anxiety is specifically organized around fear of negative evaluation in social or performance situations. Specific Phobia situational type (public transportation, enclosed spaces) does not involve social scrutiny. Distinguish by asking: is the fear about what others will think, or about the situation itself?
OCD+
Key distinguisher: OCD involves fear driven by intrusive obsessional content and compulsions to neutralize it. A client who fears contamination from touching doorknobs and washes their hands is better described by OCD than Specific Phobia. In OCD, avoidance and rituals generalize extensively; in Specific Phobia, the fear and avoidance are typically more circumscribed.
PTSD+
Key distinguisher: PTSD avoidance can be mistaken for Specific Phobia when a client avoids a specific object/situation associated with trauma (e.g., avoiding cars after a crash). The key differentiator: PTSD requires trauma exposure meeting Criterion A, intrusion symptoms, negative alterations in cognition/mood, and hyperarousal — not just avoidance of a feared stimulus. Both can co-occur.
Illness Anxiety / Somatic Symptom Disorder+
Key distinguisher: A client who fears having a serious illness and avoids medical care is not best explained by Specific Phobia — this maps to Illness Anxiety Disorder or Somatic Symptom Disorder. BII Specific Phobia is distinguished by a concrete fear of blood, injections, or medical procedures rather than preoccupation with illness itself.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~7–9% 12-month prevalence; one of the most common anxiety disorders. Animal and natural environment types are most prevalent in the general population; situational type is most common in clinical settings. Women are diagnosed at roughly 2:1 over men.
Course
Without treatment, specific phobias are typically chronic. Most develop in childhood and persist into adulthood. Functional impairment ranges from minimal (rare contact with stimulus) to severe (e.g., needle phobia affecting medical care). Many clients do not seek treatment unless the phobia interferes with important life functioning.
Clinical Notes
Exposure-based treatment (in vivo or virtual reality) is highly effective — response rates up to 90% with structured exposure. BII type requires modified technique (applied tension) due to vasovagal risk. Note: Specific Phobia is one of the most treatable diagnoses in the DSM — realistic prognosis framing at intake is important for engagement.
Common Comorbidities
Other anxiety disorders (especially GAD, Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety), MDD, Agoraphobia. Clients frequently present with multiple specific phobias — code each separately. BII type has elevated rates of pain-related disorders and specific medical avoidance patterns.

Differential Diagnoses
GAD+
Key distinguisher: Both involve anxiety and avoidance. The critical differentiator is focus: SAD anxiety is specifically tied to social evaluation and scrutiny; GAD involves multiple non-social domains (health, finances, work). If anxiety is exclusively social in nature, SAD is the primary diagnosis. Both can co-occur — and commonly do (~20–30% comorbidity).
Panic Disorder+
Key distinguisher: Both involve anxiety in social settings. Panic Disorder features unexpected panic attacks — not confined to social situations — with persistent worry about future attacks. In SAD, anxiety and panic-like symptoms occur specifically in social contexts and are driven by fear of negative evaluation, not the panic itself. SAD with panic attacks: specify the panic attack as a symptom, do not add Panic Disorder unless unexpected attacks also occur.
Agoraphobia+
Key distinguisher: Agoraphobia involves fear of situations from which escape would be difficult or embarrassing — such as crowds, public transport, or being outside alone — primarily driven by fear of incapacitation or panic, not social evaluation. SAD fear is specifically about being judged or embarrassed by others. However, overlap is common; both can be diagnosed when criteria are met.
MDD+
Key distinguisher: MDD can produce social withdrawal and avoidance that resembles SAD. The key differentiator: MDD social withdrawal is driven by anhedonia, low energy, or hopelessness — not fear of negative evaluation. SAD social avoidance persists even when mood is otherwise stable. Comorbid MDD and SAD is extremely common (~50%) and worsens prognosis for both; always assess both.
Autism Spectrum Disorder+
Key distinguisher: ASD involves difficulties with social communication and reciprocity that are pervasive and not driven by fear of negative evaluation. SAD involves adequate social skills with significant anxiety about using them. However, SAD commonly co-occurs with ASD — social anxiety in autistic individuals is well-documented and should not be dismissed as merely "ASD." Assess each independently.
Avoidant Personality Disorder+
Key distinguisher: AvPD and generalized SAD overlap so substantially that they are considered by many to exist on a continuum. AvPD criteria require pervasive patterns across relationships and self-concept (hypersensitivity to criticism, feelings of inadequacy, social inhibition since early adulthood); generalized SAD may be present with less pervasive self-concept involvement. When criteria for both are met, both diagnoses are appropriate. Do not use one to dismiss the other.
Normal Shyness / Social Discomfort+
Key distinguisher: Shyness is a temperamental trait — not a disorder. SAD requires that fear, anxiety, or avoidance causes clinically significant distress or functional impairment. Not all introverts or shy individuals meet criteria. Document functional impact carefully to justify diagnosis. Many clients with SAD present high-functioning and may minimize symptoms or normalize them.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~7% 12-month prevalence; ~13% lifetime — one of the most common anxiety disorders. More prevalent in women (~1.5–2x), though men are more likely to seek treatment. Median age of onset: 13 years; most cases begin before age 25.
Course
Often chronic without treatment. Onset typically in adolescence; many clients present in adulthood having quietly organized their lives around avoidance. Marked life constriction — career choices, relationships, and daily functioning shaped by avoidance. Spontaneous remission is uncommon.
Clinical Notes
CBT with exposure (in-vivo and imaginal) is the gold standard. ACT is increasingly supported. IFS is well-suited: many SAD presentations involve protective parts organized around shame and anticipated rejection that respond powerfully to parts work. Fear of negative evaluation and shame are core maintaining factors — address both explicitly. Avoid colluding with avoidance strategies, even subtle ones.
Common Comorbidities
MDD (~50%), GAD (~30–50%), Panic Disorder, Avoidant Personality Disorder, Alcohol Use Disorder (often as self-medication, ~20%), Specific Phobia, PTSD, Eating Disorders. Substance use comorbidity is clinically important — assess for alcohol use in particular.

Differential Diagnoses
Adjustment Disorder+
Key distinguisher: Adjustment Disorder is used when a stress response doesn't meet criteria for a more specific disorder. If full PTSD criteria are met (including the specific symptom clusters B–E), PTSD takes precedence even if symptoms arise from a stressor that might not seem "catastrophic."
MDD+
Key distinguisher: PTSD Criterion D (negative mood/cognitions) overlaps significantly with MDD. Key differentiators: PTSD requires trauma exposure and intrusion/avoidance symptoms; MDD does not require a precipitating event. Both can be diagnosed concurrently — this is common and clinically significant.
GAD / Other Anxiety Disorders+
Key distinguisher: PTSD hyperarousal can resemble GAD. The essential difference: PTSD anxiety is organized around and traceable to a specific traumatic event. GAD involves multiple, non-trauma-specific worry domains. Trauma exposure plus intrusion/avoidance symptoms clinches PTSD.
Complex PTSD / Personality Disorders+
Key distinguisher: Complex PTSD (recognized in ICD-11) captures chronic, repeated trauma with prominent affect dysregulation, negative self-concept, and relational disturbances. This overlaps considerably with CPTSD and BPD presentations. DSM-5-TR does not include Complex PTSD as a separate diagnosis — document complexity in formulation narrative.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~3.5% lifetime prevalence in the US; higher in women (2x), combat veterans, first responders, and survivors of sexual trauma. Not all trauma exposure leads to PTSD (~20% of exposed individuals).
Course
Variable. ~50% recover within 3 months without treatment. Chronic course more likely with early-life trauma, repeated exposure, lack of support, and comorbidities. Symptoms may fluctuate for years.
Clinical Notes
Always assess window of tolerance before trauma processing. CPT and PE are first-line empirically-supported treatments; EMDR is also well-supported. Stabilization precedes trauma processing for complex presentations. IFS approaches are increasingly evidence-informed for this population.
Common Comorbidities
MDD (~50%), GAD, Panic Disorder, Alcohol/Substance Use Disorders (~30–50%), BPD (especially with early/repeated trauma), Somatic Symptom Disorder, Dissociative Disorders, Chronic pain conditions.

Cyclothymia mood fluctuations are more endogenous, longer in duration, and less interpersonally reactive. Comorbidity is common and diagnostically complex.
ADHD+
Key distinguisher: ADHD often co-presents with emotional dysregulation and mood lability, but the core features are inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity — not cycling mood episodes. Carefully distinguish whether the instability is ADHD-driven or represents a separate mood pattern. Both can coexist.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~0.4–1% general population. Often goes unrecognized for years. Clients frequently report "always being this way" — chronic, ego-syntonic mood instability is easily normalized.
Course
Chronic and persistent. Significant risk of progression to Bipolar I or II (15–50%). Long-term monitoring for episode escalation is an important treatment component. Mood stabilizers are often indicated; coordinate with prescriber if not already engaged.
Clinical Notes
Clients often experience the hypomanic periods as preferred functioning and resist framing them as symptomatic. Psychoeducation about the bipolar spectrum is important. ACT is well-suited for building observer perspective on mood states without suppression. Medication coordination is often warranted.
Common Comorbidities
Anxiety disorders, Substance Use Disorders, ADHD, Personality Disorders. High overlap with BPD in clinical presentations.

Differential Diagnoses
Major Depressive Disorder+
Key distinguisher: MDD is a generalized depressive syndrome — pervasive low mood, anhedonia, cognitive changes, and neurovegetative symptoms not specifically anchored to a loss. PGD is grief-specific — centered on yearning, preoccupation with the deceased, and loss-related identity disruption. Both can coexist; if full MDE criteria are met independently of grief content, diagnose MDD as well.
PTSD+
Key distinguisher: PGD and PTSD can co-occur, particularly after traumatic loss (sudden death, violent death, suicide). PTSD involves trauma-specific intrusion, hyperarousal, and avoidance tied to the manner of death; PGD involves grief-specific yearning and preoccupation with the person themselves. In traumatic loss, assess for both.
Normal Grief+
Key distinguisher: Normal grief involves intense sadness and longing that gradually diminish and allow for reintegration into daily life. PGD is distinguished by persistence (≥12 months for adults), functional impairment, and reactions that are out of proportion to or inconsistent with cultural norms. Do not pathologize grief — only diagnose PGD when criteria are clearly and fully met.
Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~10% of bereaved individuals; higher rates following sudden, violent, or traumatic loss (~25–30%). New in DSM-5-TR — prevalence data are still emerging.
Course
Persistent without treatment. Prolonged Grief Disorder–specific therapy (PG-T), developed by Katherine Shear, is the most evidence-based approach. CBT and IPT also show benefit. ACT is well-suited for the acceptance and identity-level work at the core of PGD.
Clinical Notes
Use this diagnosis carefully — grief should not be over-pathologized. PGD is appropriate when grief has become genuinely stuck and impairing. IFS is particularly well-suited: protector parts often block access to grief, and grief work involves reconnecting with the self that existed in relation to the deceased. Suicidality should be assessed — PGD carries elevated suicide risk.
Risk Factors
Traumatic or sudden loss (suicide, accident, violence), prior attachment difficulties or insecure attachment style, low social support, history of prior losses, prior mental health conditions, dependent relationship with the deceased.

Differential Diagnoses
Illness Anxiety Disorder (Health Anxiety)+
Key distinguisher: Illness Anxiety Disorder involves high health anxiety with minimal or no somatic symptoms — the preoccupation is about having or developing a serious disease. Key distinguisher: PTSD with dissociative subtype involves depersonalization/derealization as a symptom within a broader trauma response (intrusion, avoidance, hyperarousal). Prevalence

Course

Differential Diagnoses
OCD+
Key distinguisher: BDD and OCD share the obsession-compulsion structure, but BDD obsessions are specifically about perceived physical appearance. BDD typically has poorer insight than OCD and higher rates of absent/delusional insight. Key distinguisher: SAD involves fear of social scrutiny and negative evaluation broadly. Prevalence, Course & Clinical Notes
Prevalence
~2–2.9% general population. Relatively equal in men and women (muscle dysmorphia more common in men). Often first presents to dermatology or plastic surgery settings rather than mental health.
Course
Typically chronic without treatment. Onset in adolescence. BDD is frequently comorbid with MDD and anxiety disorders. Suicidality is significantly elevated — assess at every session. Without treatment, the disorder rarely remits spontaneously.
