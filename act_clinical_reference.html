Foundation
Model Overview
What ACT is, what it aims for, and how it works
Core Aim

ACT aims to increase psychological flexibility — the ability to contact the present moment fully, as a conscious human being, and to change or persist in behavior when doing so serves valued ends. Symptom reduction is not the primary goal; values-based living is.

The core question ACT asks is: What would you do differently if your thoughts and feelings were no longer an obstacle?

Theoretical Roots

ACT is grounded in Relational Frame Theory (RFT) — a behavioral account of human language and cognition. The central premise is that human suffering is largely a product of normal language processes: we fuse with our thoughts, avoid uncomfortable inner experiences, and lose contact with the present moment. Language that helps in the external world (analysis, comparison, prediction) creates suffering when turned inward.

ACT is a third-wave behavioral therapy. It sits alongside DBT and MBCT in its emphasis on mindfulness and acceptance, but is distinguished by its focus on values and the broader philosophical framework of Functional Contextualism.

Psychological Inflexibility
  • Cognitive fusion — believed thoughts as literal truth
  • Experiential avoidance — struggle with inner experience
  • Dominance of past/future (not present)
  • Self-as-content — rigid, narrow self-story
  • Values disconnection — unclear or unowned
  • Impulsivity or persistent avoidance
Psychological Flexibility
  • Defusion — noticing thoughts without believing them
  • Acceptance — making room for inner experience
  • Present-moment contact
  • Self-as-context — the noticing self
  • Clear, owned values
  • Committed action aligned with values
ACT Stance in Session

ACT is not directive in the CBT sense. The therapist does not correct distorted thinking or argue for more rational beliefs. Instead, the therapist uses experiential exercises, metaphors, and functional analysis to shift the client's relationship to their inner experience. The question is not "Is this thought true?" but "Is responding to this thought workable? Does it help you move toward the life you want?"

The Hexaflex
Six interdependent processes, all in service of psychological flexibility
How to Read the Hexaflex

The six processes are not sequential steps — they are simultaneous, mutually reinforcing facets of psychological flexibility. Work in one area tends to support progress in others. The center (psychological flexibility) is the shared goal.

Clinically: you don't need to work all six processes in every session. You work what the client brings. Over time, a full treatment arc should touch all six.

Process Addresses Core Question
Acceptance Experiential avoidance Can you make room for this experience without fighting it?
Defusion Cognitive fusion Can you notice this thought rather than be it?
Present Moment Past/future dominance What are you noticing right now?
Self-as-Context Rigid self-as-content Who is the one noticing all of this?
Values Values disconnection What kind of person do you want to be?
Committed Action Behavioral inaction or avoidance What can you do, today, in the direction of what matters?
The FEAR–DARE Heuristic (Harris)

Russ Harris offers a clinical shorthand for movement between inflexibility and flexibility:

FEAR (Inflexibility)
  • Fusion with thoughts
  • Evaluation of experience
  • Avoidance of experience
  • Reason-giving for behavior
DARE (Flexibility)
  • Defusion from thoughts
  • Acceptance of experience
  • Responsible values-based action
  • Engagement with the present

ACT Language Guide
Terminology that belongs in ACT documentation — and what to avoid
Why Language Matters in Documentation

ACT note language should reflect ACT processes, not CBT or generic therapy language. Using CBT terms (cognitive distortions, thought records, challenging beliefs) when your treatment is ACT-based creates a documentation mismatch that can be clinically misleading and problematic in audits.

Instead of (CBT/generic) Use (ACT-aligned) Why
Challenged cognitive distortion Facilitated defusion from fused thought ACT doesn't dispute thought content
Reframed negative thought Explored relationship to thought; created distance Reframing implies changing the thought

Process 1 of 6
Acceptance
Making room for difficult inner experience without struggling against it
What It Is

Acceptance in ACT is not resignation, tolerance, or liking a difficult experience. It is the active, willful process of opening up and making room for thoughts, feelings, sensations, and urges — without unnecessary defense. The Latin root accipere means "to receive." The client learns to receive experience rather than fight it.

The target of acceptance work is experiential avoidance — the attempt to control, suppress, escape, or avoid private experiences, even when doing so leads to greater long-term suffering.

When to Work Acceptance
  • Client is fighting or fleeing an emotion ("I can't stand feeling this way")
  • Avoidance behaviors are dominant — numbing, distraction, substance use, overwork
  • Client is suffering more from their struggle against pain than from the pain itself
  • Anxiety, grief, shame, or fear are driving the presenting picture
Clinical Interventions
  • Expansion: Guiding client to breathe into the physical location of a difficult feeling rather than tightening against it
  • Naming the emotion: Slowing down to identify and label inner experience — decreases amygdala reactivity
  • Willingness vs. wanting: Clarifying that acceptance means willingness to have an experience, not wanting it
  • The struggle switch: Identifying when fighting the emotion is adding a second layer of suffering
  • Cost-benefit of avoidance: What is the avoidance costing the client in terms of their values?
Clinical Prompt
"What would it look like to make a little room for that feeling — not to like it, just to let it be there?"
Clinical Prompt
"On a scale of 0–10, how hard are you fighting this feeling right now? What does the fighting cost you?"
Somatic anchor: breath, body scan IFS link: parts that carry difficult emotions
Process 2 of 6
Cognitive Defusion
Stepping back from thoughts to notice them, rather than being fused with them
What It Is

Cognitive fusion occurs when we get caught up in our thoughts — treating them as literal truth, commands, or threats that must be obeyed or escaped. Defusion is the set of techniques that create distance between the person and their thoughts, so that thoughts can be observed rather than believed.

Critically: ACT does not dispute the content of thoughts. It shifts the client's relationship to thoughts. A fused thought says "I am worthless." A defused client notices "there's that 'I am worthless' story again."

Clinical Defusion Techniques
  • Leaves on a stream: Imagining thoughts as leaves floating past on a stream — watching them come and go without grabbing hold
  • Naming the story: "What's the name of this story your mind is telling? 'The I'm Not Good Enough story?'"
  • I'm having the thought that…: Prefacing a thought with "I'm having the thought that" to create distance ("I'm a failure" → "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure")
  • Thanking the mind: "Thanks, mind" — acknowledging the thought without acting on it
  • Singing the thought: Saying a distressing thought in a silly voice — disrupts the literal meaning without disputing it
  • Externalizing: Giving the internal critic a name or character; creating narrative distance
  • Observing the thought train: Noticing when the mind has hooked you and choosing to step off
Clinical Prompt — Noticing Fusion
"It sounds like your mind is really hooked on that thought. What would happen if you just noticed it was there, without letting it run the show?"
Clinical Prompt — Workability
"Is buying into that thought helping you move toward the life you want, or pulling you away from it?"
Distinguishing Defusion from Suppression

Thought suppression (trying not to think about something) is the opposite of defusion and typically increases the thought's frequency. Defusion does not try to eliminate the thought — it changes the client's response to the thought. This distinction is often worth naming explicitly with clients.

Process 3 of 6
Present-Moment Awareness
Flexible, voluntary contact with the here and now
What It Is

Present-moment awareness refers to flexible, voluntary, non-judgmental contact with the current moment. In ACT, this is not mindfulness as a general wellness practice — it is a specific clinical skill that enables all other ACT processes to operate. You cannot do acceptance, defusion, or values clarification without some degree of present-moment contact.

The opposite is mind wandering — being dominated by past rumination, future worry, or automatic pilot — which blocks the ability to respond flexibly to what's actually happening.

In-Session Practices
  • Grounding: Brief orienting to the physical senses — what do you see, hear, feel right now?
  • Body scan: Moving attention through the body; noticing sensation without evaluation
  • Noticing the noticing: "Who is it that's noticing all of this?" — bridges to self-as-context
  • Pause and name: In moments of strong emotion, pause the narrative and ask "what's here right now?"
  • Contact with this moment in session: "Where are you right now? Not the story — what's actually present?"
Clinical Prompt
"If you set the story aside for just a moment — what do you actually notice right now, in your body, in this room?"
Connection to Rumination and Anxiety

Rumination is past-focused; anxiety is future-focused. Both pull the client out of the present moment. In ACT, present-moment work is often the entry point for clients presenting with anxiety or depression — not as a cure, but as a platform from which to do values and defusion work.

Importantly: the goal is not to achieve an empty, peaceful mind. It is to build the capacity to return to the present when the mind has wandered — repeatedly, without self-criticism.

Process 4 of 6
Self-as-Context
The observing self — the "you" that notices thoughts, feelings, and experiences
What It Is

ACT distinguishes between self-as-content (the story, the labels, the narrative: "I am anxious," "I am broken," "I am a people-pleaser") and self-as-context (the stable, consistent observer who notices all of those experiences).

Self-as-context is sometimes called the "observer self," the "noticing self," or the "I" behind experience. It is the vantage point from which everything else is noticed. It cannot be threatened by any thought or feeling, because it is not the content — it is the container.

Why It Matters Clinically

Clients with rigid self-narratives (especially trauma survivors, perfectionists, and those with identity-level presenting concerns) often fuse heavily with self-as-content. Self-as-context work creates a stable sense of self that is not contingent on thoughts, emotions, or past experiences — which is both protective and enabling of change.

This process is often the most experiential and hardest to describe conceptually. Metaphors are more useful than explanations.

Clinical Approaches
  • The Chessboard metaphor: You are the board, not the pieces — thoughts and feelings can move freely without threatening the board itself
  • The Sky metaphor: You are the sky; thoughts, feelings, and memories are weather — passing through, never destroying
  • Observer exercise: Guided practice of noticing thoughts, feelings, sensations, and then asking "who is noticing?"
  • Continuity of self: "You've had thousands of thoughts and emotions in your life. Through all of them, there has been a 'you' who experienced them."
Clinical Prompt
"You've noticed a lot of things today — thoughts, feelings, sensations. Who is it that's been doing all that noticing?"
IFS parallel: Self as capital-S Self; parts as content Particularly relevant: trauma, identity transitions, BPD features
Process 5 of 6
Values
Freely chosen, verbally constructed directions for living
What They Are — And Are Not

In ACT, values are not goals, rules, or moral principles imposed from outside. They are freely chosen, ongoing directions — like compass headings that guide behavior regardless of outcome. You cannot achieve a value; you can only live in its direction.

Values differ from goals: "Run a 5K" is a goal (achievable, checkable); "Be someone who honors my body" is a value (ongoing, directional). This distinction is clinically important and often worth explaining directly to clients.

Common Values Domains
  • Relationships: Family, friendship, intimacy, community
  • Work and contribution: Meaningful work, creativity, service
  • Personal growth: Learning, education, self-development
  • Health and body: Physical vitality, self-care
  • Leisure and play: Enjoyment, adventure, rest
  • Spirituality / meaning: Connection to something larger
  • Citizenship: Community, environment, social justice
Values Clarification Techniques
  • Values cards sort: Client ranks/sorts cards with values words into meaningful categories
  • Tombstone / eulogy exercise: "What do you want people to say about you? What kind of person do you want to have been?"
  • At your best: "Think of a time when you were truly at your best. What were you doing? What were you honoring?"
  • Pain as values compass: "What does this pain tell you about what matters to you? You don't suffer about things that don't matter."
  • Relationship values: "What kind of partner/friend/parent/colleague do you want to be?"
  • Naming in session: When values emerge organically in session, naming them explicitly ("It sounds like connection is really central for you")
Clinical Prompt — Values from Pain
"You're really hurting around this. What does that pain tell you about what genuinely matters to you?"
Clinical Prompt — Clarifying Goals vs. Values
"If you achieved that goal tomorrow — what would it give you? What would it mean about who you are?"
Distinguishing Owned vs. Imposed Values

Clients sometimes present with values that belong to family systems, religious tradition, or social norms rather than to themselves. ACT holds that values must be freely chosen to be clinically useful. The therapist's job is not to impose new values, but to help the client distinguish what they truly choose from what they've been told they should want. This is often identity-level work — handle with care.

Process 6 of 6
Committed Action
Building patterns of effective action guided by values
What It Is

Committed action is the behavioral arm of ACT — the process of building larger and larger patterns of effective, values-consistent behavior. It is not about willpower or motivation. It is about taking the next step in the direction of what matters, especially in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings.

Committed action is distinguished from avoidance-based behavior change. The client is not changing behavior to feel better — they are changing behavior because it's consistent with who they want to be.

Clinical Components
  • Values-to-goals bridge: Translating values into concrete, achievable goals and action steps
  • Behavioral activation: Identifying and scheduling values-consistent behaviors — often overlaps with BA for depression
  • Urge surfing: Staying with an urge or impulse without acting on it, or acting on a competing values-consistent behavior
  • Barriers identification: What thoughts and feelings show up as obstacles? How will the client respond?
  • Small steps: Committed action begins where the client is, not where they wish they were
  • Reviewing follow-through: Non-judgmentally exploring what happened with action commitments; problem-solving obstacles
Clinical Prompt — Linking Action to Values
"What's one small thing you could do in the next week that would be consistent with that value — even if the anxiety comes along for the ride?"
Clinical Prompt — Anticipating Barriers
"What do you think your mind will say when you try to do that? How will you respond to it?"
On Relapse and Recommitment

ACT reframes failure to follow through not as weakness, but as a normal part of behavior change requiring recommitment — not self-criticism. The key question after a "lapse" is: "What got in the way? What thoughts or feelings hooked you? And what matters enough to try again?"

This stance is self-compassion-compatible and reduces shame spirals that sabotage forward motion.

IFS link: check for parts that block committed action — fear, shame Documentation: name the specific action step and values connection

Key Metaphors
Core ACT metaphors organized by primary process
Using Metaphors in ACT

ACT relies heavily on metaphor because direct instruction is less effective than experiential learning when working with language-based fusion. A well-placed metaphor creates a new relationship to experience that the client can return to outside of session. Use them naturally — don't introduce them as exercises unless the client is ready for structured work.

Acceptance — The Unwanted Guest
Imagine hosting a party. An uninvited guest shows up — someone you really don't want there. You can spend the whole night trying to throw them out, hiding them from other guests, arguing with them in the corner. Or you can let them in, acknowledge them, and still have your party.
Use for: experiential avoidance; clients who fight hard against emotions
Defusion — Leaves on a Stream
Imagine sitting beside a gently flowing stream. As thoughts arise, imagine placing each one on a leaf and watching it float downstream. You don't have to hold it or push it away — just watch it pass.
Use for: cognitive fusion; anxious rumination; intrusive thoughts
Defusion — The Mind as Storyteller
Your mind is a storytelling machine. It's been telling stories your whole life — some helpful, some not. You can notice the story without being trapped inside it. You are not the story. You are the one the story is being told to.
Use for: self-narrative fusion; "I am broken/worthless/unlovable" stories
Self-as-Context — The Chessboard
Imagine a chessboard. The black pieces and white pieces are fighting — representing conflicting thoughts, feelings, parts of yourself. Now: which side are you rooting for? Actually, you are the board. You hold all the pieces. No piece can threaten the board itself.
Use for: internal conflict; self-as-content fusion; identity rigidity
Self-as-Context — The Sky
You are the sky. Thoughts, feelings, memories, urges — these are weather. Storms come through. Clouds gather. Sun breaks. The sky itself is never harmed by any of it. The weather changes; the sky remains.
Use for: emotional overwhelm; trauma; clients who feel "taken over" by emotion
Acceptance / Values — Tug-of-War with a Monster
Imagine you're in a tug-of-war with a monster at the other end of a rope. You're pulling hard, terrified of being pulled into the pit. Here's the thing — the monster can go on forever. Your arms will give out. What if you just… dropped the rope? The monster is still there. But now your hands are free.
Use for: chronic avoidance struggle; exhaustion from fighting anxiety or depression
Values — The Compass
A compass doesn't tell you when you'll arrive anywhere. It tells you which direction you're heading. Values work the same way — they're not destinations, they're directions. You can always move in the direction of what matters, regardless of how far away the destination feels.
Use for: introducing values vs. goals distinction; demoralized clients

  • "Where are you right now — not the story, but this moment?"
  • "What do you notice in your body as you say that?"
  • "Can we slow down for a second? What's happening right now, underneath the words?"
  • "Your mind seems to be in [the past / the future]. What's here, right now?"
Self-as-Context
  • "You've noticed a lot of things today — who has been doing all the noticing?"
  • "That's a thought you're having. Who is the one having it?"
  • "You've had this feeling for years. Through all of it, there's been a 'you' experiencing it. What's that like to sit with?"
  • "If the anxious part of you is a character — who's the one watching that character?"
Values
  • "What does this pain tell you about what really matters to you?"
  • "If you could live the next year according to what you most deeply care about, what would be different?"
  • "What kind of person do you want to be in this relationship / at work / in your body?"
  • "When were you most fully yourself? What were you doing?"
  • "What are you doing when you feel like life is worth living?"
  • "What are your goals with this [situation]? And underneath those — what matters to you about that?"
Committed Action
  • "What's one small step you could take this week in the direction of that value?"
  • "What thoughts and feelings will show up when you try to do that? How will you respond?"
  • "Even if the anxiety comes with you — what would you do?"
  • "What got in the way this week? What hooked you? And what would you do differently?"
  • "Not 'can you' — will you?"

ACT + IFS Integration
Where these frameworks converge, diverge, and work together
The Case for Integration

ACT and IFS are not competing models — they address overlapping but distinct clinical terrain. ACT provides a behavioral/functional framework for changing the relationship to inner experience. IFS provides a relational, parts-based framework for understanding why the inner experience is organized the way it is. Together, they cover more ground than either alone.

The integration works best when ACT provides the what (defusion, acceptance, values) and IFS provides the who (which part is speaking, what does it need, what burden does it carry).

IFS Language
  • Parts (managers, firefighters, exiles)
  • Self (capital-S) — calm, curious, compassionate
  • Burdens — what parts carry
  • Blending — parts taking over
  • Unblending — accessing Self
  • Retrieval — unburdening exiles
ACT Parallel
  • Thoughts, feelings, urges, sensations
  • Self-as-context — the observing self
  • Fused beliefs, experiential avoidance
  • Cognitive fusion
  • Defusion, present-moment contact
  • Acceptance, committed action
Clinical Application Points
  • Entry: Use present-moment grounding (ACT) to help the client access Self-energy before IFS parts work
  • Fusion: When a part blends, use defusion techniques to create distance — "Let's just notice that part is really loud right now"
  • Avoidance: When a manager part is blocking, acceptance work can help the client make room rather than fight the block
  • Values: After IFS work with an exile, values clarification (ACT) helps the client rebuild forward direction now that the burden is lighter
  • Committed action: Parts that block action (fear, shame) can be addressed through IFS; the action steps themselves are then planned through ACT
  • Self-as-context: Reinforce that Self in IFS and the observing self in ACT are functionally the same — stable, continuous, not threatened by parts
Caution on Mixing Language in Session

Clients do not need to understand both models. Use whichever language lands better for the individual client. Some clients take naturally to parts work; others find the ACT framing more accessible. You can use IFS internally as a clinical map while using ACT language in session, or vice versa. The integration is for the clinician, not necessarily the client.

Outcome Measures — ACT-Relevant

  • VQ (Valued Living Questionnaire) — assesses values importance and progress
  • PHQ-9 / GAD-7 — standard symptom measures; useful for tracking functional impairment alongside ACT process measures
  • PCL-5 — for PTSD presentations
