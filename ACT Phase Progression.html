ACT Therapy:
A Session-by-Session Arc

From Intake Through Termination
Tended Path Counseling  ·  Bethany Sherman, LMHC  ·  April 2026
A note on structure

ACT does not have a rigid phase sequence. The six hexaflex processes are present throughout treatment — what changes is which process is foregrounded, and why. The arc described here reflects the logic and typical sequencing of ACT as described by Hayes, Strosahl & Wilson (2012) and Luoma, Hayes & Walser (2007), not a lock-step protocol. Clinical judgment, client presentation, and the therapeutic relationship always take precedence.

Where synthesis and inference appear

Well-established content from the ACT literature is presented without qualification. Where the framing reflects clinical synthesis, integration with IFS, or application to specific presentations, it is marked as such. IFS integration notes are clinical synthesis — not drawn from an established published ACT/IFS protocol.

The Shape of ACT Treatment

ACT is a transdiagnostic, process-based therapy. Its goal is not symptom reduction per se — it is to increase psychological flexibility: the ability to contact the present moment fully, and to persist in or change behavior in service of what matters. Symptoms may decrease as a consequence, but that is not the primary mechanism or target.

The treatment arc moves broadly from establishing safety and context (Phase 1), through undermining unworkable control strategies (Phase 2), toward building the internal capacities — defusion, acceptance, self-as-context, present-moment awareness — that allow values-based committed action to become sustainable (Phases 3 through 6). Termination is explicitly framed as part of the clinical work, not an administrative endpoint.

The Six Core Processes

The hexaflex processes are not sequential — they operate simultaneously, with different processes foregrounded at different phases of treatment.

1 Phase
Intake and Assessment
Sessions 1–2
Functional understanding, safety, therapeutic alliance
Primary Goals
  • Establish safety, rapport, and the therapeutic frame
  • Conduct a functional assessment — not just diagnostic (what is the client doing, and what function does it serve?)
  • Map the client's primary areas of suffering and the control strategies they have used to manage it
  • Begin to orient the client to ACT's model of change without full didactic explanation
  • Identify working diagnosis and complete required clinical documentation (90791)
Clinical Focus

ACT assessment is functional, not primarily categorical. Beyond DSM criteria, you are asking: What does this person avoid — externally and internally? What do they do to manage their discomfort, and is it working? What have they lost or restricted in service of controlling their experience? What do they care about that they can no longer access?

This is also where you assess for psychological flexibility directly — some clients are already fairly decentered and values-oriented; others are profoundly fused and avoidant. The latter need more foundational work before values and committed action will be accessible.

The alliance itself is a clinical instrument in ACT. Early warmth, curiosity, and non-judgment model the observing stance before you ever name it.

Key Assessment Domains
  • Presenting concerns and history — functional, not just descriptive
  • Experiential avoidance: what internal states are being avoided, and how
  • Cognitive fusion: which thoughts carry the most weight, most believability
  • Behavioral restriction: what the client no longer does because of internal states
  • Values domain: what matters, even if currently unreachable
  • Prior treatment history and its effect on the client's relationship to change
  • Safety — SI/SH screen, crisis history, current risk
IFS Integration — Synthesis

The intake is also an opportunity to notice what parts are present — the part that came seeking help, protective parts that might be reluctant or skeptical, and any early indication of exile-level pain. You are not doing parts work in session 1; you are attending.

2 Phase
Creative Hopelessness
Sessions 2–4
Undermining the unworkable change agenda
Primary Goals
  • Surface and examine the client's current approach to managing suffering
  • Demonstrate — through experience rather than argument — that the control agenda is not working or has hidden costs
  • Create enough discomfort with the status quo that willingness to try something different emerges naturally
  • Do this without nihilism, false reassurance, or implying the client has been doing it wrong
Clinical Focus

This is one of the most delicate phases in ACT and the one most frequently either skipped (in favor of jumping to values and action) or done clumsily (in a way that the client experiences as being told they are wrong or stupid for trying to feel better). The goal is not hopelessness about the client — it is creative openness to a different relationship with suffering. The word "creative" is load-bearing.

The primary technique is asking the client to tell you what they have tried, and then exploring what happened. You are not arguing against their strategies — you are following their experience with them. The client arrives at the limitation of control strategies through their own reflection, not through your persuasion.

Core Questions
  • What have you tried to feel less [anxious / depressed / ashamed]? How has that worked — short term and over time?
  • If you could make this feeling go away forever, would you want to? What would you be free to do?
  • Has trying not to feel this way ever made it smaller, or has it made your life smaller?
Useful Metaphors
  • The Tug of War with the Monster — fighting versus dropping the rope
  • Quicksand — the more you struggle, the deeper you sink
  • The Man in the Hole — digging harder with the same shovel
Hexaflex Processes Most Active
Acceptance — introducing willingness as alternative to control Present-Moment Awareness — noticing what the struggle actually costs, right now
IFS Integration — Synthesis

From an IFS frame, creative hopelessness is partly about recognizing that protective managers have been working very hard with good intentions — and that their strategies may be creating burdens of their own. The invitation is to curiosity about those parts, not defeat. This reframe tends to land more gently with clients who would experience "your strategies aren't working" as shaming.

3 Phase
Defusion and Acceptance
Sessions 3–8
Changing the relationship to internal experience
Primary Goals
  • Help the client experience thoughts as thoughts — not as facts, commands, or threats
  • Build willingness to contact difficult internal states without suppression, escape, or excessive defense
  • Introduce mindfulness practices that support both defusion and acceptance
  • Distinguish acceptance from resignation, defeat, or liking the experience
Cognitive Defusion

Defusion interventions target the function of thoughts — specifically, the degree to which language is controlling behavior in unhelpful ways. You are not trying to change the thought's content or challenge its accuracy. You are changing the client's relationship to the thought.

  • Naming the story: "There's the 'I'm fundamentally broken' story again."
  • Thanking the mind: "Thanks, mind" — used with warmth, not dismissively
  • Saying it in a silly voice or very slowly — creates experiential distance without argument
  • Physicalization: Where is this thought located? What does it look like?
  • Leaves on a stream, clouds in the sky — observing thoughts as passing events
  • The passengers on the bus metaphor — thoughts as passengers, client as driver
Acceptance / Experiential Willingness

Acceptance in ACT is an active, chosen stance toward internal experience — not passive endurance. It is making room for feelings, sensations, memories, and urges without unnecessary defense. The clinical question underneath acceptance work is always: "Is avoiding this experience costing you something that matters?"

  • Expanding around the feeling rather than eliminating it
  • Physicalizing sensations — size, shape, weight, temperature — to defuse their urgency
  • The Chinese finger trap metaphor
  • Surf the urge — riding sensations with curiosity rather than acting on or suppressing them
Mindfulness Introduction

The primary function of mindfulness in ACT is attentional flexibility — the ability to be here, noticing experience without being controlled by it. Short practices (2–3 minutes) embedded in session are more effective than assigning lengthy practices for homework.

Common Stalls at This Phase
  • Client intellectualizes — can describe defusion beautifully but doesn't use it experientially; redirect to in-session practice
  • Client confuses acceptance with giving up, especially with depression or chronic pain; clarify explicitly
Therapist drift to watch for: Using workability questions effectively, but slipping into "let's examine whether that thought is accurate." That's CBT, not defusion. The corrective is staying with the question of function — does holding this thought tightly work for the client's life?
Documentation note

When documenting defusion or acceptance work, the audit-ready frame is: what does this intervention allow the client to do? Not: what does it reduce? "Client practiced defusion from the thought 'I am a burden' and was able to initiate contact with a family member" — not "client challenged distorted cognition."

IFS Integration — Synthesis

Defusion and acceptance work often reveals parts — and clients sometimes experience a frightened or overwhelmed part during acceptance exercises. Having an IFS frame available allows you to name what's happening ("It sounds like a part of you really doesn't want to let that feeling in — which makes sense. Can we get curious about that part?") rather than pushing through. This can actually deepen acceptance rather than derailing it.

4 Phase
Self-as-Context
Sessions 5–9
Building the stable observing self
Primary Goals
  • Help the client distinguish between the observer of experience and the content of experience
  • Build access to a stable, continuous perspective that contains thoughts, feelings, and stories without being identical to them
  • Reduce identity fusion — particularly important for clients with chronic depression, trauma history, or rigid self-concept
Clinical Focus

Self-as-context is the conceptually deepest process in ACT, and often the one that produces the most durable change for clients doing identity-level work. The observer self is the "I" that has been present throughout the client's life — across roles, moods, ages, experiences. It is not a different self; it is the perspective from which all experience is witnessed.

Self-as-content is the fused, over-identified position — "I am anxious," "I am broken," "I am the trauma that happened to me." Self-as-context is the riverbank, stable and containing, while thoughts and feelings are the river and its contents. (Note: directionality matters — self-as-context is the bank, not the river.)

Key Exercises and Metaphors
  • The Observer Exercise — guided meditation tracking the stable "I" across changing experiences
  • The Chessboard Metaphor — pieces (thoughts, feelings) move; the board is always there
  • The Sky and Weather Metaphor — weather changes; the sky remains
  • "Notice that you are noticing" — simple in-session pointing to the observer
  • Continuity of self across time: "Who was aware of your 10-year-old self's experiences? The same 'I' that is here now."
Presentations That Warrant Extra Care
  • Dissociative presentations — the observer exercise can feel destabilizing; ground first, go slowly
  • Complex trauma with disrupted identity — a continuous self may feel foreign; start with small, concrete examples
  • Clients in acute grief — "who am I without my role" may compound loss; hold both
IFS Integration — Synthesis

Self-as-context and IFS Self-energy are overlapping constructs that complement each other directly. Both describe a stable, observing, compassionate center distinct from any particular part or internal state. Clients who have difficulty accessing self-as-context through ACT's observer exercises sometimes find IFS language more accessible. The two models reinforce each other here without tension.

5 Phase
Values Clarification
Sessions 6–12
Finding direction from the inside out
Primary Goals
  • Help the client identify chosen values — directions, not destinations — that are genuinely their own
  • Distinguish values from goals, rules, obligations, and inherited expectations
  • Connect the client's suffering to their values (the things that hurt us most are connected to what we care about most)
  • Build motivation for committed action that does not depend on feeling ready or comfortable
Clinical Focus

Values clarification is often the most transformative phase for clients doing identity-level work. It is also where the most significant clinical errors occur — specifically, when clients present values that are actually avoidance strategies in disguise ("I value peace" = I avoid conflict), or when therapists accept socially desirable values without exploring whether they are genuinely chosen.

Values in ACT are ongoing directions — they cannot be achieved or completed, only lived. This is a crucial distinction. A goal is "I want to reconnect with my daughter." The value underneath it might be "being a present and loving parent." The value persists even if the goal becomes unattainable.

Key Values Clarification Exercises
  • Funeral / obituary exercise — what do you want people to say? Who do you want to have been?
  • Valued Living Questionnaire (VLQ) or Bulls-Eye Values Survey — structured domain mapping
  • The 80th birthday letter
  • "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail — and if your anxiety/depression came with you?"
  • Guided experiential writing about a peak experience — what values were expressed?
Distinguishing Genuine Values from Inherited or Avoidance-Based Ones
  • If no one ever knew you were living this way, would you still choose it?
  • Does this feel chosen, or does it feel like something you're supposed to want?
  • Is this something that genuinely matters to you, or is it a rule you've been following?
Preferred framing — inherited vs. imposed values

"Inherited values" is more clinically and culturally accurate than "imposed values." It preserves the acknowledgment that family and cultural transmission are real — sometimes beautiful, sometimes constraining — and resonates with the IFS concept of legacy burdens. It avoids implying that family systems are simply oppressive.

Common Stalls at This Phase
  • Client cannot access values — often a sign of significant fusion or depression; work with whatever tiny flickers of caring are still present
  • Client's values are all relational or other-directed — explore what the client cares about for themselves, gently
  • Client becomes overwhelmed by the gap between where they are and their values — this is grief work, and it is appropriate; don't bypass it
IFS Integration — Synthesis

Some clients' stated values are voices of managers or internalized expectations from family systems. Helping the client listen for the difference between a value that comes from the core self versus one that comes from a protective part is nuanced, identity-level clinical work. The IFS language of legacy burdens maps directly onto inherited values.

6 Phase
Committed Action
Sessions 8–ongoing
Building behavioral patterns from values
Primary Goals
  • Help the client develop and sustain patterns of behavior in service of their values — even in the presence of difficult internal states
  • Build psychological flexibility in action: persist, shift, or change behavior as circumstances require, without abandoning values
  • Identify and work through barriers (internal and external) that interrupt values-based action
  • Develop skills for handling relapse and getting back on track without shame spirals
Clinical Focus

Committed action is not willpower — it is flexible, values-anchored behavior across contexts. The primary distinction from behavioral activation in CBT is that the goal is not to feel better in order to act; the goal is to act in service of what matters, bringing whatever internal states are present along for the ride.

Committed action requires defusion, acceptance, and self-as-context to be sufficiently developed — otherwise, barriers will reliably pull the client off course and the committed action assignments will fail, which can deepen shame and hopelessness. This is why committed action, though present in early sessions, cannot be the primary early focus.

Structure of Committed Action Work
  • Identify the value being served
  • Name a specific goal or action step in that direction
  • Anticipate the internal barriers (thoughts, feelings, urges) that will show up
  • Plan how to respond to those barriers using ACT processes — not eliminate them
  • Set a time frame; review the following session
  • If the action was not taken: explore with curiosity, not judgment — what showed up? What would have helped?
Common Barriers and ACT Responses
  • "I'll do it when I feel better" — defusion from the feeling-first assumption; values don't require readiness
  • "I tried and it didn't work" — clarify what "working" means; was the action taken? Was it values-aligned?
  • "What's the point" — fusion with hopelessness; acceptance of the feeling while reconnecting with values
  • Avoidance disguised as planning — gently name the pattern, don't shame it
IFS Integration — Synthesis

When committed action consistently stalls, it is often because a protective part — a manager or firefighter — is blocking. The IFS question is not "how do I push through this barrier?" but "what is this part afraid would happen if the client moved toward this value?" Getting the answer often unlocks the block more effectively than behavioral intervention alone.

Present-Moment Awareness

Mindfulness and present-moment contact are not a "phase" — they are the medium through which all other ACT work occurs. In-session, the therapist models present-moment awareness through attention, silence, and willingness to notice what is happening in the room.

As treatment matures, present-moment awareness becomes less about formal practices and more about a general orientation — the client spontaneously notices when they have gone to autopilot, catastrophizing, or avoidance, and can return to the present without it being a dramatic event.

Psychoeducation

ACT is minimally didactic — you are not teaching the hexaflex to clients. However, brief, transparent explanations of the model are appropriate when clients ask how or why you are doing something. The key is to keep psychoeducation experiential whenever possible: try it rather than talk about it.

T Phase
Termination
Final 3–4 sessions
Consolidation, self-as-guide, and the transition
Primary Goals
  • Consolidate gains — not just "look how far you've come" but "what do you now know about how to do this?"
  • Transfer the therapeutic function to the client's own ACT skills and values compass
  • Normalize setbacks and build a specific relapse-response plan
  • Address the relational meaning of ending — for both client and therapist
  • Frame termination itself as an act of committed action and values-based living
Clinical Focus

ACT termination is values-forward. The question is not "are you symptom-free?" but "do you know how to live in service of what matters, even when things are hard?" A client who leaves treatment with persistent anxiety but who can carry it into valued living has succeeded in ACT. A client who leaves symptom-free only because life has been smooth has not yet been tested.

The termination phase should explicitly review the client's own ACT processes — which defusion techniques were most useful? What does acceptance feel like now versus when treatment began? What are their clearest values? What committed actions will they continue?

Relapse Response Planning
  • Name the specific thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that signal the client is getting pulled back into avoidance or fusion
  • Identify what "getting back on track" looks like — specific, not vague
  • Establish criteria for returning to treatment ("if X happens, I will reach out within Y days")
  • Normalize return to treatment — it is not a failure; it is self-aware use of a resource
The Termination Relationship

The ending of the therapeutic relationship has genuine weight. The client may have grief, anxiety, ambivalence, or relief — and all of those are welcome in the room. The therapist's task is to model the same willingness to contact that experience fully — without rushing toward reassurance or closure.

Termination handled with presence and care is itself a model of ACT: making contact with difficulty, staying with what matters, and acting from values even when it is uncomfortable.

Criteria for Readiness to Terminate
  • Client demonstrates functional flexibility across multiple life domains
  • Client can identify and use at least 2–3 ACT processes independently when distress arises
  • Client's values are explicit and accessible — they know what they are orienting toward
  • Client has a working relapse-response plan
  • Termination is collaborative and agreed upon, not unilateral

Session Count and Duration

ACT research has been conducted in formats ranging from single sessions to 40+ sessions. In standard outpatient private practice:

Brief / Focused
8–16
Specific phobia, adjustment disorder, single domain
Moderate Complexity
16–30
GAD, MDD with adequate premorbid functioning, life transition
Complex / Open-Ended
30+
Chronic depression, trauma history, identity-level work, attachment disruption

Billing code 90837 (53+ minutes) is most appropriate for ACT given the experiential, process-based nature of the work. Shorter sessions (90834 / 90832) may be appropriate in stabilization phases or toward the end of treatment.
